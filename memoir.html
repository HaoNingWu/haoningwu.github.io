A Mathematical Memoir
Math, People, and Ideas
Hao-Ning Wu
A continuing record of my mathematical life
Last updated: July 2026
This is a continuing account of how I came to mathematics, how particular questions entered my work, and how conversations, accidents, failed arguments, and unexpected connections gradually shaped my research.
Every theorem has a history that usually disappears from the published paper. Behind each finished argument are conversations, unsuccessful attempts, misconceptions, and moments of unexpected clarity. This memoir records some of those histories.

I
An Accidental Beginning
I did not choose Jinan University in 2015 because I had already decided to become a mathematician. One rather practical attraction was that, unlike most Chinese universities at the time, Jinan did not require first-year students to undergo the compulsory military training that was then common across the country. It was an almost accidental decision. Looking back, however, it was one of the most consequential decisions I have ever made. It led me to my first research project in approximation theory and, ultimately, to the mathematical questions that have remained with me ever since.
I first met Congpei An in the autumn of 2016, when he was an associate professor at the math department. One thing immediately caught my attention: unlike most mathematics courses at the time, his Numerical Linear Algebra course used an English-language textbook. Curious, I enrolled. The textbook was Trefethen and Bau's Numerical Linear Algebra[1]. Instead of weekly assignments, students were asked to choose ten chapters of the book and copy them by hand. An called this the "Kunihiko Kodaira method of learning."
The following spring of 2017, I took his Numerical Approximation course, based on Powell's Approximation Theory and Methods[2]. The homework remained the same.
Around the same time, together with two classmates, I applied for the National Undergraduate Innovation and Entrepreneurship Program under his supervision. When he heard that we wanted him to advise a project, his first question was, “Do you know the Wang–Xiang formula?” We did not. Our project, Barycentric Interpolation: Algorithms and Applications, became my first experience with mathematical research.
That summer, on my own, I copied another book, Trefethen's Approximation Theory and Approximation Practice[3], amid the humid heat of Guangzhou.
II
Regularization
An assigned us two papers to read: the celebrated paper on barycentric interpolation by Berrut and Trefethen[4], and a paper by Wang and Xiang connecting Legendre barycentric weights with Gauss–Legendre quadrature weights[5].
At first, I understood these problems mainly through formulas and numerical experiments. Over time, I began to see that the central issue was not merely how to construct an approximation, but why a discrete collection of samples could represent a continuous function space stably.
III
Hong Kong
I began my doctoral study at the University of Hong Kong under the supervision of Xiaoming Yuan. My work there gradually moved between approximation theory, optimization, and numerical analysis for partial differential equations.
I continued to collaborate with Congpei An on hyperinterpolation and related problems, but the context had changed. I was no longer working only within an undergraduate project. I was beginning to formulate questions independently, develop proofs, write numerical codes, and understand how individual problems might belong to a broader mathematical program.
IV
Beyond Exactness
A decisive change in my thinking began after I attended an online lecture by Lloyd N. Trefethen and read his writing on the limitations of polynomial exactness as a measure of quadrature quality.
I began to wonder whether the exact cubature assumption used in hyperinterpolation was genuinely essential, or whether it was only a convenient sufficient condition.
Is algebraic exactness the mathematical mechanism behind stability, or merely one way of obtaining it?
An online lecture by Lloyd N. Trefethen that prompted me to question the role of algebraic exactness.
At the time, Congpei was the person with whom I most regularly discussed questions in spherical approximation, so I naturally shared this question with him. I worked on the problem for some time and developed much of the argument, but one essential stability step remained unresolved.
After several rounds of discussion, Congpei suggested that a Marcinkiewicz–Zygmund inequality might provide the appropriate replacement for exactness. This observation allowed the analysis to move forward. I then developed the main framework, completed the principal proofs and numerical experiments, and prepared the first full draft.
Only later did I realize that an anonymous referee of one of our earlier papers had already remarked that an MZ inequality might be used in place of algebraic exactness. We had not pursued that suggestion at the time.
In retrospect, the project emerged from several distinct sources. Trefethen's critique prompted the initial question. Congpei recognized the relevance of MZ inequalities at a critical stage. The earlier referee report showed that the underlying possibility had already been noticed. My own role was to turn these ingredients into a sustained analysis and a complete mathematical framework.
V
Thinking Spectrally
Over time, I found myself approaching many problems more naturally through spectral structure than through spatial localization. Spherical harmonics, polynomial expansions, spectral multipliers, and operator eigenstructures became a common language connecting problems that had initially appeared unrelated.
This viewpoint gradually carried me from hyperinterpolation toward Galerkin methods, integral equations, partial differential equations, and sampling-based numerical analysis.
Sampling determines what information is available. Spectral structure determines how that information moves through a numerical method.
VI
The Problems I Now Want to Pursue
My current work is increasingly organized around a single question: how do weak, imperfect, or nonstandard sampling assumptions propagate through numerical methods for continuous problems?
This includes stable approximation from scattered data, quadrature without exact cubature, Galerkin and Nyström discretizations, integral equations on spheres and surfaces, frame constructions, and spectral neural operators.
I do not regard this as a finished research program. It is still changing, and this page will change with it.
