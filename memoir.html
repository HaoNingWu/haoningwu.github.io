Math, People, and Ideas
Hao-Ning Wu
A continuing record of my mathematical life
Last updated: July 2026
This is a continuing account of how I came to mathematics, how particular questions entered my work, and how conversations, accidents, failures, and unexpected connections gradually shaped my research.
Every theorem has a history that usually disappears from the published paper. Behind each finished argument are conversations, unsuccessful attempts, misconceptions, and moments of unexpected clarity. This memoir records some personal histories.

I
An Accidental Beginning
I did not choose Jinan University in 2015 because I had already decided to become a mathematician. One rather practical attraction was that, historically oriented toward overseas Chinese students, Jinan then did not require freshmen to undergo the compulsory military training that was common across the country. It was an almost accidental decision. Looking back, however, it was one of the most consequential decisions I have ever made. It led me to my first research project in approximation theory and, ultimately, to the mathematical questions that have remained with me ever since.
I first met Congpei An in the autumn of 2016. His Numerical Linear Algebra course caught my attention because it used an English-language textbook, which was unusual among the mathematics courses available to us at the time. Curious, I enrolled. The textbook was Trefethen and Bau's Numerical Linear Algebra[1]. Instead of weekly assignments, students were asked to choose ten chapters of the book and copy them by hand. An called this the "Kunihiko Kodaira method of learning."
The following spring of 2017, I took his Numerical Approximation course, based on Powell's Approximation Theory and Methods[2]. The homework remained the same. Around the same time, together with two classmates, I planned to apply for the National Undergraduate Innovation and Entrepreneurship Program and asked An to supervise us. His first question was, “Do you know the Wang–Xiang formula?” We did not. The resulting project, Research on barycentric interpolation: algorithms and applications, was my first encounter with mathematical research.
That summer, on my own, I copied another book, Trefethen's Approximation Theory and Approximation Practice[3], by hand. I would not now recommend copying books as a general method of learning mathematics. But at that age, with plenty of time and no clear direction, it gave my days a certain structure and gradually drew me deeper into the subject.
II
Regularization
An assigned us three papers to read: the survey on barycentric interpolation by Berrut and Trefethen[4], the paper by Wang and Xiang connecting Legendre barycentric weights with Gauss–Legendre quadrature weights[5], and the paper by Glaser, Liu, and Rokhlin on the fast computation of Gauss–Legendre quadrature nodes and weights[6].
He then gave us two tasks. One was to reproduce the algorithm of Glaser, Liu, and Rokhlin. The other was to explore whether the Wang–Xiang formula could be generalized from Legendre points to more general Jacobi points. We failed to reproduce the algorithm, and a few months later I discovered that the second problem had already been solved by Wang, Huybrechs, and Vandewalle several years earlier[7].
By the time the project had passed its midpoint, I felt, as the team leader, obliged to do something for the completion report. I turned to An’s doctoral dissertation[8], which was based on two papers he had written with Chen, Sloan, and Womersley. One concerned the computation of spherical t-designs, whose associated quadrature rules possess polynomial exactness[9]; the other used the resulting point sets for L2-regularized discrete least-squares approximation and derived an explicit solution[10]. By then I had also learned about Gauss quadrature and its remarkable degree of exactness. I tried to adapt the same line of thought to polynomial approximation on [−1,1]. I derived closed-form solutions and error estimates for an L2-regularized approximation method and then formulated an L1-regularized counterpart. After obtaining these results, I showed them to An, who suggested developing the work into a paper.
Writing the first paper was difficult. I prepared an initial manuscript and posted it on arXiv in May 2018 so that my undergraduate project could be formally completed. We did not submit it to a journal until April 2019, when we sent it to the Journal of Scientific Computing. Three months later, after two sharply conflicting reports, the manuscript was rejected. The IMA Journal of Numerical Analysis then desk-rejected it within days because the English was not ready for review, while inviting us to resubmit after the manuscript had been thoroughly rewritten. It was the summer after my undergraduate graduation, also the ancient era without GPTs. I spent the next month rewriting the manuscript with Higham's Handbook of Writing for the Mathematical Sciences[11] almost constantly at my side. The writing still seems poor to me today, but it was close to the best I could produce at the time. We submitted the revised version, and this time it was sent out for review. A few months later, another pair of conflicting reports arrived. One referee pointed out that our analysis of the L2-regularized method relied on an incorrect use of the Christoffel--Darboux formula, invalidating the subsequent result, while the L1-regularized part remained intact. The paper was rejected again.
After the second rejection, we separated the manuscript into two papers. I rebuilt the L2-analysis and refocused the paper on Tikhonov-regularized least-squares approximation, which eventually appeared in Inverse Problems[12]. I continued developing the L1 direction into what we called Lasso hyperinterpolation, extending the L1-regularized approximation to general domains. The numerical experiments turned out to be much harder than the theory, and the resulting paper was later published in the SIAM Journal on Scientific Computing[13]. The mathematics in both papers now seems modest to me, but I still remember my heartbeat when the acceptance email from Inverse Problems arrived. It was December 2020, and I was waiting for my order in a McDonald's on Hill Road in Hong Kong.
III
Springback and Enhanced TV
Hong Kong had already become my home by then. I had moved there in September 2019 to begin my PhD at the University of Hong Kong under the supervision of Xiaoming Yuan. I believe An spoke highly of me to Yuan. At the time, as is still the case today, Jinan University was not a typical source of PhD students for the university. In addition to the standard admission procedures, Yuan provided additional justification to the Faculty of Science in support of my admission, and part of my funding came from his research grant. I did not fully appreciate these details at the time, but looking back, I understand that he took a chance on me at an important stage of my life.
Yuan gave his students considerable freedom in choosing their research directions. By the time I joined him, his senior PhD student Yongcun Song and postdoc fellow Hangrui Yue were already exploring the optimal control of PDEs with him, a direction that Yuan himself had not previously worked on. I met Yuan on my very first day in Hong Kong. Song suggested that I wait outside the room where they were about to have a discussion, in case I had a chance to meet Yuan beforehand. When Yuan arrived and saw me, he took me to his own office for a brief conversation. We talked for about ten minutes. He first asked how I was settling into life in Hong Kong amid the social unrest at the time, and then encouraged me to find a research direction that suited my own interests. What I remember most was his advice that I should not pursue optimization with him, despite it being his established field. He explained that if we published optimization papers together, people might naturally attribute the main credit to him rather than to me, making it harder for me to establish an independent research identity. I did not fully appreciate the significance of this advice at the time, but I have often thought back on it with gratitude in the years since.
Despite this freedom, he expected all his students to understand the foundations of optimization and the ideas underlying his own work. Shu Gong, a student who had joined him a year ago, and I alternated every two weeks in presenting chapters from Boyd and Vandenberghe’s Convex Optimization[14], followed by Nocedal and Wright’s Numerical Optimization[15]. Like many students, I tended to relax during the weeks when I was not presenting and prepare intensely when my turn approached. We eventually worked through both books from beginning to end.
Around this time, An approached me about writing another paper on regularized least-squares approximation. The idea traced back to my undergraduate years, when An had sent me a paper by Esser, Lou, and Xin introducing the L1-L2 and L1/L2 regularizers[16]. He simply suggested that I read it, and nothing more came of it at the time. By then, however, my first two papers had already shaped the way I approached approximation problems. I found myself looking for formulations that admitted closed-form solutions rather than iterative optimization. Since the L1-L2 regularizer did not naturally lead to such a formulation, I suggested considering L1-L22 instead. An found the idea interesting, and I eventually wrote a manuscript on least-squares approximation based on this regularizer, inviting Yuan to join the project. However, I soon realized that the analysis differed little from our earlier work on Lasso hyperinterpolation[13]. It was also around this time that I began to lose interest in research driven primarily by replacing one regularizer with another. Such work often felt more like repetition than genuinely new mathematics.
After reading a series of papers by Lou and her collaborators on compressed sensing with the L1-L2 model, I decided to study the corresponding L1-L22 model. An had no tracked records on compressed sensing at the time, while Yuan's expertise lay primarily in optimization algorithms for solving such models rather than in their reconstruction analysis. As a result, I largely had to find my own way through the project.
This was still before the era of generative AI. I worked through the mathematical analysis in the L1-L2 compressed sensing paper by Yin, Lou, He, and Xin[17] line by line, gradually adapting their arguments to the L1-L22 setting. For the numerical experiments, I downloaded the code accompanying their paper from Lou's webpage and modified every parts involving the L2 term into their L22 counterpart, learning the implementation largely by trial and error. The writing remained a struggle as well. Grammarly had become part of my routine by then, and I would go through its suggestions one by one in manual editing mode, trying to understand every correction rather than simply accepting it. Around this time, I also came up with the playful name "springback" for the model, borrowing a term from metal forming. The intuition was that while the L1​ term shrinks both noise and signal, subtracting L22​ allows the signal to "spring back" slightly. After completing the first draft, I sent it to An and Yuan.
An followed his usual practice of printing out the manuscript, writing brief comments in the margins, and sending me photographs of the annotated pages. Yuan, however, soon identified an issue in the optimization algorithm. For a constrained optimization problem, he explained, one could not simply move the constraint into the objective as a penalty term and then solve the resulting unconstrained problem. The constraint had to be retained, with each iteration projected back onto the feasible set. He also invested considerable effort in improving my writing. Although Grammarly had greatly reduced my grammatical errors and typos, Yuan pointed out that the logical structure of my exposition still needed work. We revised the manuscript through many rounds. He eventually called me into his office and went through the introduction with me sentence by sentence, asking what a reader would expect after each statement, what information was missing, and which sentences had to be moved for the argument to unfold coherently. The manuscript was eventually accepted by Applied and Computational Harmonic Analysis[18] after one round of revision.
