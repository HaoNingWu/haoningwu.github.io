This is a continuing account of how I came to mathematics, how particular questions entered my work, and how conversations, accidents, failures, and unexpected connections gradually shaped my research.
Every theorem has a history that usually disappears from the published paper. Behind each finished argument are conversations, unsuccessful attempts, misconceptions, and moments of unexpected clarity. This memoir records some of those histories.

I
An Accidental Beginning
I did not choose Jinan University in 2015 because I had already decided to become a mathematician. One rather practical attraction was that, historically oriented toward overseas Chinese students, Jinan then did not require freshmen to undergo the compulsory military training that was common across the country. It was an almost accidental decision. Looking back, however, it was one of the most consequential decisions I have ever made. It led me to my first research project in approximation theory and, ultimately, to the mathematical questions that have remained with me ever since.
I first met Congpei An in the autumn of 2016. His Numerical Linear Algebra course caught my attention because it used an English-language textbook, which was unusual among the mathematics courses available to us at the time. Curious, I enrolled. The textbook was Trefethen and Bau's Numerical Linear Algebra[1]. Instead of weekly assignments, students were asked to choose ten chapters of the book and copy them by hand. An called this the "Kunihiko Kodaira method of learning."
The following spring of 2017, I took his Numerical Approximation course, based on Powell's Approximation Theory and Methods[2]. The homework remained the same. Around the same time, together with two classmates, I plan to apply for the National Undergraduate Innovation and Entrepreneurship Program and asked An to supervise us. His first question was, “Do you know the Wang–Xiang formula?” We did not. The resulting project, Research on barycentric interpolation: algorithms and applications, was my first encounter with mathematical research.
That summer, on my own, I copied another book, Trefethen's Approximation Theory and Approximation Practice[3], by hand. I would not now recommend copying books as a general method of learning mathematics. But at that age, with plenty of time and no clear direction, it gave my days a certain structure and gradually drew me deeper into the subject.
II
Regularization
An assigned us three papers to read: the survey on barycentric interpolation by Berrut and Trefethen[4], the paper by Wang and Xiang connecting Legendre barycentric weights with Gauss–Legendre quadrature weights[5], and the paper by Glaser, Liu, and Rokhlin on the fast computation of Gauss–Legendre quadrature nodes and weights[6].
He then gave us two tasks. One was to reproduce the algorithm of Glaser, Liu, and Rokhlin. The other was to explore whether the Wang–Xiang formula could be generalized from Legendre points to more general Jacobi points. We failed to reproduce the algorithm, and a few months later I discovered that the second problem had already been solved by Wang, Huybrechs, and Vandewalle several years earlier[7].
By the time the project had passed its midpoint, I felt, as the team leader, obliged to do something for the completion report. I turned to An’s doctoral dissertation[8], which was based on two papers he had written with Chen, Sloan, and Womersley. One concerned the computation of spherical t-designs, whose associated quadrature rules possess polynomial exactness[9]; the other used the resulting point sets for L2-regularized discrete least-squares approximation and derived an explicit solution[10]. By then I had also learned about Gauss quadrature and its remarkable degree of exactness. I tried to adapt the same line of thought to polynomial approximation on [−1,1]. I derived error estimates for an L2-regularized approximation method and then formulated an L1-regularized counterpart. Only after obtaining these results did I show them to An, who suggested developing the work into a paper.
Writing the first paper was difficult. I prepared an initial manuscript and posted it on arXiv in May 2018 so that my undergraduate project could be formally completed. We did not submit it to a journal until April 2019, when we sent it to the Journal of Scientific Computing. Three months later, after two sharply conflicting reports, the manuscript was rejected. The IMA Journal of Numerical Analysis then desk-rejected it within days because the English was not ready for review, while inviting us to resubmit after the manuscript had been thoroughly rewritten. It was the summer after my undergraduate graduation, also the ancient era without GPTs. I spent the next month rewriting the manuscript with Higham's Handbook of Writing for the Mathematical Sciences[11] almost constantly at my side. The writing still seems poor to me today, but it was close to the best I could produce at the time. We submitted the revised version, and this time it was sent out for review. A few months later, another pair of conflicting reports arrived. One referee pointed out that our analysis of the L2-regularized method relied on an incorrect use of the Christoffel--Darboux formula, invalidating the subsequent result, while the L1-regularized part remained intact. The paper was rejected again.
After the second rejection, we separated the manuscript into two papers. I rebuilt the L2-analysis and refocused the paper on Tikhonov-regularized least-squares approximation, which eventually appeared in Inverse Problems[12]. I continued developing the L1 direction into what we called Lasso hyperinterpolation, extending the L1-regularized approximation to general domains. The numerical experiments turned out to be much harder than the theory. I began with one-dimensional examples, then adapted code that An had obtained from Atkinson for the disk and code from his doctoral work for the sphere. The cube, however, had no code to inherit, and I spent many weeks building everything from scratch. The resulting paper was later published in the SIAM Journal on Scientific Computing[13]. The mathematics in both papers now seems modest to me, but I still remember my heartbeat when the acceptance email from Inverse Problems arrived. It was December 2020, and I was waiting for my order in a McDonald's on Hill Road in Hong Kong.
Hong Kong had already become my home by then. I had moved there in September 2019 to begin my PhD at the University of Hong Kong under the supervision of Xiaoming Yuan. I believe An spoke highly of me to Yuan, who also went out of his way to make my admission possible. At the time, as is still the case today, Jinan University was not a typical source of PhD students for the university. Yuan had to provide additional justification to the Faculty of Science for my admission, and part of my support came from his own research grant. I did not fully appreciate these details at the time, but looking back, I understand that he took a chance on me at an important stage of my life.

Beyond Exactness
A decisive change in my thinking began after I attended an online lecture by Lloyd N. Trefethen and read his writing on the limitations of polynomial exactness as a measure of quadrature quality.
